Python Sales EDA — Turning Raw Transactions into Revenue Strategy

₹5.01MTotal Revenue
1,000Transactions Analysed
14Feature Variables
–31%Avg. Profit Margin

This end-to-end Python pipeline — built in Jupyter Notebook using Pandas, NumPy, Matplotlib, and Seaborn — ingested a 1,000-row, 14-column transactional sales dataset and transformed it into a boardroom-ready strategic narrative. The analysis surfaced a critical structural finding: despite ₹5.01M in gross revenue, the business recorded a mean profit of –₹58,822 per transaction, driven not by weak top-line performance, but by a fundamentally broken cost-to-revenue structure demanding immediate executive intervention.

  • Dataset: 1,000 rows × 14 features — zero missing values; production-ready after datetime parsing and numeric coercion
  • Engineered 5 derived KPIs: Total_Revenue, Total_Cost, Profit, Profit_Margin, and Discount_Band
  • Revenue seasonality confirmed — strong peaks in Jan, Aug, Oct & Nov; dips in Feb, Jul & Sep signal promotion windows
  • Clothing leads revenue (₹1.31M) yet records the largest category loss (–₹16.26M), exposing a volume-without-margin trap
  • North region generates the highest revenue (₹1.37M) but also the deepest loss (–₹15.18M) — requires cost audit priority
  • Correlation heatmap: Unit_Cost ↔ Total_Cost = 0.68; Profit driven by cost control, NOT revenue growth
  • 20–50% discount band generates peak revenue (₹1.75M, 341 transactions) — but profitability impact remains unresolved
  • Discount has near-zero correlation (r = 0.02) with Total_Revenue — current discount strategy needs a structural rethink

📈 Key Data Insights

  • Revenue Seasonality: Monthly revenue holds stable between ₹3.6L–₹4.6L with confirmed peaks in January, August, October, and November — supporting high-confidence demand forecasting and pre-emptive inventory builds for peak windows
  • Category Performance Paradox: Clothing generates the highest revenue (₹13.13L) yet records the deepest loss (–₹1.63Cr). Furniture mirrors this — volume does not equal margin in this business model
  • Regional Imbalance: North and East lead revenue but shoulder the heaviest losses (–₹1.52Cr and –₹1.52Cr respectively). South's relatively smaller loss (–₹1.36Cr) despite lowest revenue suggests better cost efficiency in the region
  • Discount Band Analysis: The 20–50% band drives peak revenue (₹17.52L, 341 transactions) and highest average transaction volume. However, the 0–1% band with only 53 transactions raises the question of whether minimal-discount transactions represent premium or distress buys
  • Profit Distribution Pathology: The profit histogram skews heavily left — most transactions cluster between –₹2,00,000 and –₹20,000. Boxplot outliers confirm isolated but extreme loss events pulling the mean below –₹58,000
  • Quantity Sold Distribution: Near-uniform histogram (1–49 units) confirms no single buyer concentration risk. The business serves a broad, regular-consumption customer base — not a wholesale-dependent model

💡 Strategic Recommendations

  • Immediate Cost Audit (Priority 1): Unit_Cost is the single most destructive variable (r = –1.00 with Profit). Management must initiate a supplier renegotiation and procurement review across all four product categories before scaling volume
  • Discount Strategy Overhaul: Since discounts show near-zero correlation (r = 0.02) with revenue, the current blanket discount policy is both ineffective and margin-dilutive. Shift to targeted, segment-specific promotions in Feb, Jul, and Sep dip windows only
  • Category Prioritisation: Food (₹12.02L revenue, –₹1.25Cr loss) demonstrates the best loss-to-revenue ratio. Management should investigate Food's supply chain for transferable cost-control practices applicable to Clothing and Furniture
  • Seasonal Inventory Planning: Leverage the confirmed Jan, Aug, Oct, Nov peaks to pre-position inventory and negotiate bulk purchase discounts — converting stable demand into procurement leverage
  • Regional Efficiency Benchmarking: South region's relatively contained loss despite lowest revenue suggests an operationally leaner model. North and East should be benchmarked against South's cost structure for root-cause variance analysis
  • Pricing Decoupling: Unit_Price ↔ Unit_Cost correlation of 1.00 indicates pure cost-plus pricing with no value-based premium. Introducing category-specific price elasticity testing can unlock margin without volume dependence
SPSS Site Selection Advisory — Business Research Methods
⚡ Quick Glance Summary

SPSS Site Selection Study — Mall vs. Downtown for Business Clothing in Delhi/NCR

N = 61Survey Respondents
p = .009Chi-Square Significance
65.6%Discriminant Accuracy
7 TestsSPSS Methods Applied

This quantitative research study — conducted as a Business Research Methods end-term project — applies a comprehensive multi-method IBM SPSS analytical framework to answer a commercially critical site selection question: where should a business clothing retailer open in Delhi/NCR? The study deploys Chi-Square, Independent Samples T-Test, One-Way ANOVA, Pearson Correlation, Multidimensional Scaling (MDS), Factor Analysis, and Discriminant Analysis on 61 structured survey responses — producing a statistically grounded retail advisory with actionable site recommendations for format-specific market entry.

  • 57.4% prefer Shopping Malls vs. 42.6% Downtown — split commercially validates both formats as viable entry points
  • Chi-Square (p=.009): Shopping priority significantly determines location choice — Assortment drives Mall; Price drives Downtown
  • T-Test (p=.041): Price & Value is the only statistically significant perceptual differentiator between the two groups
  • ANOVA confirms age as the key demographic segmentor — Age 20–26 skews Downtown; Age 27–35 skews Mall
  • MDS model achieves near-perfect fit (Stress = 0.0048, RSQ = 0.9998) — three perceptual clusters: Experience, Convenience, Value
  • Factor Analysis (KMO = .631) extracts two latent dimensions explaining 73.8% of total variance: Experiential Quality → Mall; Product Value → Downtown
  • Discriminant Analysis correctly classifies 65.6% of respondents using Price (.644) and Assortment (.580) as top predictors
  • Key conclusion: Location preference is value-proposition driven — what you sell determines where you should be

🎯 Research Context & Business Problem

Delhi/NCR retailers entering the business clothing segment face a high-stakes site selection decision with no empirical consumer data to guide them — they rely on intuition, not evidence. This study provides that missing data layer by asking a precise commercial question: Which retail format — Shopping Mall or Downtown/High-Street — do consumers prefer for business clothing, and what perceptual attributes drive that preference?

  • Shopping Malls studied: Select Citywalk, Ambience Mall, DLF Promenade — anchored on parking, climate control, and curated foot traffic
  • Downtown locations studied: Connaught Place, Nehru Place, South Extension — anchored on metro access, competitive pricing, and CBD credibility
  • Research Gap: No prior empirical study maps consumer perceptual attributes specifically to business clothing retail site selection in this geography

📊 Test 1 — Chi-Square: Shopping Priority vs. Location Choice

Purpose: Does a consumer's top shopping priority (Assortment, Accessibility, Atmosphere, Price, or Service) significantly determine their location format preference?

  • Result: SIGNIFICANT — χ²(4) = 13.24 | p = .009 | p < 0.05 → H₀ Rejected
  • 54.3% of Mall shoppers cite Product Assortment as their #1 priority — "variety under one roof"
  • 57.7% of Downtown shoppers cite Price & Value as their #1 priority — "best deals, multi-brand"
  • Managerial Implication: A retailer's value proposition must match the location's dominant consumer priority. Assortment-focused brands belong in malls; price-competitive brands belong downtown

📊 Test 3 — One-Way ANOVA: Demographic Segmentation

  • By Age Group (3 of 5 significant):
  • Product Assortment: F = 4.337, p = .018 ✓ — Age 27–35 favors Mall assortment
  • Accessibility: F = 3.802, p = .028 ✓ — Age 20–26 favors Downtown accessibility
  • Price & Value: F = 5.073, p = .009 ✓ — Age 20–26 rates Downtown better on price; Age 27–35 rates Mall better
  • Store Atmosphere: p = .485 ✗ | Service Quality: p = .182 ✗ — Not significant by age
  • By Occupation: All 5 variables non-significant (p range: .088–.891) — Job status does NOT predict location preference
  • By Budget: Assortment p = .037 ✓ — Economy shoppers favor Mall assortment (M=2.15) over Mid-Premium (M=2.86)
  • Verdict: Age is the definitive demographic segmentor. Younger consumers (20–26) are downtown-oriented; older professionals (27–35) are mall-oriented

📊 Test 4 — MDS & Correlation: Perceptual Clustering

  • MDS Model Fit: Stress (Kruskal's) = 0.0048 (Excellent <0.05) | RSQ = 0.9998 (~100% variance explained) | N = 61
  • Three perceptual clusters identified in 2D Euclidean space: Experience (Atmosphere + Service, left quadrant) | Convenience (Assortment + Accessibility, right-centre) | Value (Price, standalone far-right)
  • Shoppers form perceptual bundles — not isolated ratings. Malls own Experience + Convenience; High Streets own Price
  • Pearson Correlations: Atmosphere ↔ Service: r = .619*** (strong) | Assortment ↔ Accessibility: r = .467*** (moderate) | Atmosphere ↔ Price: r = .034 (near-zero — completely independent dimensions)
  • Strategic Implication: A retailer can aggressively compete on price downtown without any risk to its atmosphere ratings — these are orthogonal consumer perceptions

💡 Evidence-Based Site Selection Recommendations

  • For Assortment-Focused Retailers: Open in Shopping Malls — Select Citywalk or Ambience Mall. Target Age 27–35 professionals with curated product range depth and premium in-store experience. Chi-Square (p=.009) and Factor Analysis both validate the Experiential Quality positioning for this format
  • For Price-Competitive Retailers: Open Downtown — Connaught Place or South Extension. Target the Age 20–26 workforce-entry cohort with transparent pricing, multi-brand variety, and metro-accessible locations. T-Test (p=.041) confirms price perception is significantly better downtown
  • Invest in Service — Universally: Atmosphere-Service correlation (r=.619) means improving service quality yields direct improvements in atmospheric perception — the highest-leverage single investment for any format
  • Omnichannel Bridge: 57.4% mall preference vs. 42.6% downtown signals near-equal market viability for both formats. A phased dual-format entry — Mall flagship + Downtown value outlet — can capture both preference segments simultaneously
  • Age-Segmented Communication: Given ANOVA confirms age as the definitive segmentor, marketing campaigns must be audience-bifurcated: aspirational lifestyle messaging for 27–35 mall shoppers; value-transparency messaging for 20–26 downtown shoppers
  • Study Limitations to Note: N=61 limits statistical power; convenience sample over-represents PGDM students; cross-sectional design does not capture seasonal preference shifts. Recommend expanding to N=200+ with stratified sampling for board-level deployment

Skilled Sapiens serves students, working professionals, and entrepreneurs through: live experiential learning, leadership skill development, one-on-one personalized mentorship, structured placement support, and entrepreneurial guidance. Mentors include IIM/IIT alumni, senior corporate professionals, and entrepreneurs who act as personal guides, not just instructors.

  • Limited repeat access to learner cohorts
  • No standardized partnership engagement models or KPIs

Abstract

This qualitative research explores the multifaceted impact of Green HRM practices on the long-term sustainability of organizations and the well-being of employees. The study investigates how sustainable HR practices contribute to creating a positive work environment, fostering a green workforce, and promoting long-term employee retention. A systematic literature review and Bibliometric Analysis using R Package (Biblioshiny) synthesize existing knowledge on the relationship between Green HRM and organizational outcomes.
